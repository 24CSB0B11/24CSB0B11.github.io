The Western Ghats, also known as the Sahyadri mountain range, is a UNESCO World Heritage site that runs parallel to the western coast of India. Stretching over 1,600 kilometers through six states (Gujarat, Maharashtra, Goa, Karnataka, Kerala, and Tamil Nadu), the Western Ghats are known for their rich biodiversity, stunning landscapes, and significant ecological importance. Here's a detailed description:

Geographical Features

  • Highest Peak: Anamudi in Kerala, standing at 2,695 meters (8,842 feet)
  • Width: Varies from 30 to 50 kilometers (19 to 31 miles)
  • Length: Approximately 1,600 kilometers (990 miles)

Biodiversity

  • Protected Areas: Numerous national parks, wildlife sanctuaries, and reserve forests, such as Silent Valley National Park, Periyar Wildlife Sanctuary, and Bandipur National Park.
  • Endemic Species: About 325 globally threatened species and many endemic species, including the Lion-tailed Macaque, Nilgiri Tahr, and Great Indian Hornbill.
  • Flora and Fauna: The Western Ghats are a biodiversity hotspot, home to an estimated 7,402 species of flowering plants, 139 mammal species, 508 bird species, 179 amphibian species, 6000 insect species, and 290 freshwater fish species.
